What are the most common dental myths?

When it comes to oral health, there are lots of misconceptions out there. Some people still believe that brushing is optional, or that certain foods cause cavities. While many of these myths aren’t true, others aren’t quite as silly as you’d assume. In fact, many of them are rooted in common misunderstandings about oral hygiene and dental care.For example, are you aware that cavities are caused by bacteria in the mouth, not by what you eat? Or that brushing does nothing to help you avoid gum disease? If so, read on for some helpful information about common dental myths so you can stop spreading them in the future!

Brushing is important, but not as important as you think.

First of all, let’s dispel a common misconception about teeth: they’re not meant to be yellow and brown. That’s the color of bacteria that cause bad breath, and if you let it go too far, it can lead to cavities, gum disease, and other serious issues.To keep them that perfect shade of white, it’s important to brush and floss daily and see your dentist at least once a year. While brushing is almost always necessary, you don’t have to use a toothbrush and toothpaste made specifically for teeth.In fact, most toothpaste is just as effective with a scrub brush and baking soda, which is far gentler on your gums than most toothbrushes.

Brushing and flossing aren’t enough.

Even with the best brushing and flossing techniques, you’re still going to have some build-up on your teeth. That’s why it’s important to visit your dentist every six months for a thorough cleaning. While you can certainly do some things at home, like use baking soda to scrub your teeth, a professional cleaning will remove more build-up and get at the root of those issues so you don’t have problems in the future.If you’re particularly concerned about your teeth, you can even talk to your doctor about getting them checked out. Be aware that this is only necessary if there are specific symptoms that concern you.

Flossing isn’t necessary, either.

As we mentioned above, you’re going to have some build-up on your teeth no matter what. However, if you don’t floss, you’re going to have trouble getting all that build-up out of your teeth and gums.While you don’t have to floss every day, you should do it at least once a week. If you don’t floss regularly, you’re more likely to experience gum irritation, toothaches, and tooth loss. That’s why so many people brush their teeth and use floss at the same time.

Gum disease isn’t a problem for everyone.

Many people think that gum disease is something that only older people get, but that’s not true. While gum disease is more common in adults, it’s also possible to get it when you’re a teen or young adult.Gum disease is caused by bacteria that grows in your gums, which can cause them to become inflamed and sore. If left untreated, it can lead to tooth loss.

A single cavity doesn’t mean anything.

You might be surprised to learn that a single cavity is almost always a sign of a larger problem. While it’s common for a single cavity to go away on its own, it’s far more likely that there’s a problem deeper in your teeth that could lead to bigger issues down the road.If you notice a cavity forming, don’t ignore it. Take quick action and make an appointment with your dentist as soon as possible. You don’t want a problem to get out of control.

You should only go to the doctor if you’re in pain.

Many people don’t think twice about going to the doctor unless they’re in pain, but that’s not the case with teeth. Yes, your teeth can get painful, but that doesn’t mean you should visit the doctor unless you’re in agony.That’s because it’s not uncommon for people to ignore the warning signs of a toothache and keep brushing it away. If you have a toothache, though, it’s important to visit your dentist as soon as possible so they can figure out what’s wrong and recommend a solution.

Bottom line

At the end of the day, it’s important to remember that there are no silly myths when it comes to dental hygiene. You should brush and floss daily, visit your dentist every six months, and don’t hesitate to see a doctor if you’re in pain.While these tips are a great place to start, the most important thing is to remember that every little decision you make has a big impact on your oral health.
