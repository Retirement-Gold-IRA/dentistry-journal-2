Dental Implants Explained
Dental Implants
Dental Implants are a way to replace missing teeth. They are more comfortable and successful than traditional tooth extractions. The procedure is not painful and will often be performed under a local anaesthetic. However, patients should expect some soreness in the mouth after the procedure. They may also experience some swelling or pain for a few days.

While conventional dental bridges may last 10 years or more, dental implants are designed to last for a lifetime. The titanium prosthetics integrate into the jawbone, making them a more permanent and effective replacement for missing teeth. Furthermore, they are non-toxic and won't be rejected by the body. Dental implants can also improve your self-esteem, because they look and function like your real teeth.

Dental implants are generally a two-stage process. In the first stage, a titanium post is surgically implanted in the jawbone. The implant post is shaped to mimic the root of a natural tooth. This prevents the jawbone from becoming eroded and provides a secure foundation for the crown restoration. In order to ensure the implant's success, Dr. Morton carefully plans its angle and placement. Over a few months, the post will fuse with the jawbone, providing a secure base for the final crown.

The process can take anywhere from one to three months. Patients can often return to their normal diet within 6 months. In addition, there are several types of dental implants to choose from. Your dentist will be able to recommend the best option for your needs. In most cases, the procedure can be performed in an outpatient setting using local anesthesia.

While dental implants are generally painless, the initial surgical procedure may cause some minor discomfort. You may experience minor bleeding around the site of the implant. Your dentist may prescribe pain medication to ease the discomfort. Your diet should be restricted to soft foods for a few days after the procedure. During this time, you may need to have stitches removed.

A dental implant is a titanium post that fits into the jawbone like a regular tooth root. It replaces a single tooth or several adjacent teeth, or all the teeth in a jaw. The number of implants required depends on the type of restoration and the amount of bone available in the jaw. Today, most implants are made from titanium, a biocompatible metal that has many advantages, including strength and durability. In the future, zirconium will also be used as an implant, but it is not yet ready for general use.

There are three main types of dental implants: narrow body implants, mini dental implants, and wide platform dental implants. The former type is the safest type, while the latter is the most complicated. Although they require a healthy jawbone, endosteal dental implants are ideal for the majority of patients.
